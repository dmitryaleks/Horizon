# The Art and Science of Probabilistic Estimation

*A deep dive into the ideas behind Horizon*

---

## The Estimation Problem

There is a particular moment in software development that almost everyone recognizes. The team is gathered — perhaps around a conference table, perhaps in a video call with a grid of faces — and someone asks the question that has launched a thousand uncomfortable silences: "How long will this take?"

What follows is a ritual. A developer tilts their head, considers the feature in question, mentally walks through the components that need to change, and arrives at a number. "About five days," they say, with varying degrees of confidence. The project manager writes it down. The sprint is planned. Everyone moves on.

But something curious happens between that moment and the moment the task is actually finished. The five days become seven. Or nine. Or, in spectacular cases, three weeks. And yet, at the next planning meeting, the same ritual plays out again with the same quiet confidence, as though the previous miss were an anomaly rather than part of a deep and well-documented pattern.

This is not a failure of intelligence or diligence. It is a failure of method. Human beings are remarkably bad at estimating how long complex tasks will take, and they are bad at it in specific, predictable, and well-studied ways. We consistently underestimate. We anchor on the best-case scenario. We forget to account for the interruptions, the edge cases, the dependencies that only become visible once you're already knee-deep in the work. Psychologists have a name for this: the planning fallacy. And it is not a bug in a few careless individuals — it is a feature of how human cognition works.

The planning fallacy, first described by Daniel Kahneman and Amos Tversky in 1979, is the tendency to underestimate the time, costs, and risks of future actions while simultaneously overestimating their benefits. It operates even when you know about it. Even when you have experienced the exact same kind of miss before. The mechanism is subtle: when asked to estimate a task, you naturally think about that specific task — its particular requirements, its particular technical challenges — and you construct a mental narrative of how the work will go. This narrative is, almost by definition, a best-case scenario. It is the version of events where nothing unexpected happens, where every dependency is available when you need it, where the code compiles on the first try, where the requirements don't change.

The problem is not that developers are optimists by nature, though many are. The problem is structural. A single-point estimate — "five days" — contains no information about uncertainty. It does not tell you whether the estimator means "definitely five days, I've done this exact thing before" or "somewhere between three and twelve days, depending on what we find." It strips away the richness of the estimator's actual knowledge and compresses it into a single number that communicates false precision.

The consequences of this systemic optimism compound in ways that are rarely visible to the people making the estimates. A project manager who hears "five days" plans accordingly — scheduling downstream work, making commitments to stakeholders, allocating resources. When the task takes eight days instead, the ripple effects propagate outward: the next task starts late, the sprint goal is missed, the release date slips. And because each individual miss seems small and explainable — "there was an unexpected edge case," "the API documentation was wrong," "I got pulled into an incident" — the systemic pattern remains invisible. Each overrun has a plausible local explanation, which makes it easy to believe that the next estimate will be accurate. It never is, not reliably, because the problem is not with the explanations but with the process.

This is the gap that probabilistic estimation aims to fill. Instead of asking for one number, it asks: given what we know about how tasks like this have gone in the past, what is the range of likely outcomes? Instead of a point, it produces a distribution. Instead of false certainty, it offers calibrated uncertainty. And it does this not by asking humans to be better at guessing, but by replacing the guessing with data.

That is the fundamental premise behind Horizon, and behind the family of techniques it draws on. The human provides the context — what the task is, how big it seems, what their initial gut estimate is — and the machine does what machines do best: it looks at the historical record, runs thousands of simulations, and reports what the data suggests. The result is not a single number but a range: there is a ten percent chance it will take this few days, a fifty percent chance it will fall below this threshold, and a ninety percent chance it will be done by this many days. Three numbers instead of one, and each one anchored in evidence rather than hope.

But to understand why this works — and why it works the way it does — we need to trace the intellectual lineage of several ideas that converge in this approach. Each one emerged from a different field, in a different decade, to solve a different problem. Together, they form a remarkably coherent framework for reasoning about uncertainty in software projects.

## Reference Class Forecasting — Kahneman's Insight

In the early 1970s, Daniel Kahneman was asked to lead a team of academics in Israel tasked with designing a new curriculum for teaching judgment and decision-making in high schools. The team had been working for about a year and had made solid progress. During one meeting, Kahneman asked each member to estimate how long it would take to finish the textbook they were writing. The answers clustered around two years, give or take a few months.

Then Kahneman turned to a colleague, Seymour Fox, who had extensive experience with similar curriculum development projects. He asked Fox a different question: not how long this particular project would take, but how long projects like this one typically took. Fox thought about it and admitted that, of the teams he knew of that had attempted similar work, about forty percent had never finished at all. Of those that did finish, none had taken less than seven years.

There was a long silence. The team members looked at each other. They had just estimated two years for a type of project that historically took at least seven and often failed entirely. And they had done so with genuine confidence, based on their honest assessment of how the work was going.

This anecdote, which Kahneman would later recount in his Nobel Prize lecture and in his bestselling book "Thinking, Fast and Slow," illustrates the distinction between what he called the inside view and the outside view. The inside view is what happens naturally when you think about a specific task: you consider its particular details, build a mental model of how the work will unfold, and derive an estimate from that model. The outside view is what happens when you step back and ask: what has actually happened when people attempted similar things in the past?

The inside view is seductive because it feels more informed. You know things about your specific task that generic statistics cannot capture — the particular technical challenges, the skill of the team, the clarity of the requirements. But this apparent advantage is consistently overwhelmed by the biases it introduces. When you think about your specific task, you are constructing a scenario, and scenarios are naturally optimistic. You imagine the path that works, not the dozen paths that don't.

The outside view, by contrast, is stubbornly empirical. It does not care about the narrative you've constructed. It simply asks: when people have estimated tasks of this size in the past, how far off were they? And the data, across decades of research, is remarkably consistent. People underestimate. Not always, not by the same amount, but on average and in aggregate, the actual duration exceeds the estimate.

The Danish planning researcher Bent Flyvbjerg took this insight and ran with it, applying reference class forecasting to major infrastructure projects. He studied hundreds of transportation projects around the world and found that cost overruns were not exceptional — they were the norm. Rail projects ran over budget by an average of forty-five percent. Bridges and tunnels by thirty-four percent. Roads by twenty percent. And the pattern held regardless of geography, decade, or the sophistication of the estimators. Flyvbjerg argued that the only reliable corrective was to ground estimates in the actual outcomes of comparable past projects — to force the outside view into a process that naturally defaults to the inside view.

This is precisely what Horizon does, adapted to the scale of software development tasks rather than megaprojects. When you ask Horizon to estimate a five-story-point task, it does not try to reason about the task's internal details. Instead, it looks at every five-point task (and nearby-sized tasks) your team has completed in the past, examines how those tasks' actual durations compared to their estimates, and uses that historical record as the basis for forecasting. It embodies the outside view mechanically, removing the human tendency to construct optimistic narratives.

The elegance of this approach is that it gets better over time without any explicit tuning. As your team completes more tasks and adds them to the historical dataset, the reference class grows richer and more representative. If your team has a persistent tendency to underestimate large tasks but is accurate on small ones, the model will learn this pattern — not because anyone told it to, but because the data reflects it. The model does not need to understand why your team underestimates; it only needs to observe that they do, and by how much.

There is also a subtlety in how Horizon defines "similar." Kahneman's original formulation of reference class forecasting left the definition of the reference class somewhat open — what counts as a comparable project? In megaproject planning, Flyvbjerg used project type (rail, road, bridge) as the primary classifier. In software estimation, the natural classifier is task size, measured in story points. A five-point task exists in a different estimation regime than a thirteen-point task, not just because it involves less work, but because the nature of the estimation challenge is different. Small tasks tend to have more predictable scope; large tasks are more likely to harbor unknown unknowns. By weighting the reference class by story-point proximity, Horizon implements a nuanced version of Kahneman's outside view — one that respects the reality that not all past tasks are equally informative about the task at hand.

## The Bootstrap — Efron's Elegant Trick

In 1979, a Stanford statistician named Bradley Efron published a paper that would fundamentally change how scientists think about uncertainty. The paper was titled "Bootstrap Methods: Another Look at the Jackknife," and despite its modest title, it introduced an idea so powerful and so general that it reshaped the practice of statistics across virtually every field of science.

To understand what Efron was solving, you need to appreciate the problem that statisticians faced at the time. The classical tools of statistical inference — confidence intervals, hypothesis tests, standard errors — were built on assumptions about the shape of the data. If your data came from a normal distribution, you had excellent tools for quantifying uncertainty. If it came from a few other well-studied distributions, you could often find appropriate techniques. But if your data came from an unknown distribution — which is the common case in real-world problems — you were in trouble. You could assume normality and hope for the best, or you could develop custom mathematical solutions for each specific case, which was technically demanding and often impractical.

Efron's insight was disarmingly simple. Suppose you have a sample of data — say, thirty numbers — and you want to understand how uncertain your estimate of the mean is. Traditionally, you would use a formula that assumes the data is normally distributed. Efron proposed an alternative: treat your sample as though it were the entire population, and repeatedly draw new samples from it by randomly selecting data points with replacement. Each resampled dataset gives you a slightly different estimate of the mean. Do this a thousand times, and the variation across those thousand estimates tells you how uncertain your original estimate is — without any assumption about the distribution shape.

The term "with replacement" is important and deserves unpacking. When you draw a sample with replacement, you pick a data point, note its value, put it back, and then pick again. This means the same data point can appear multiple times in a single resample, and some data points might not appear at all. Each resample is the same size as the original dataset, but it contains a different mix of the original values. This random remixing is what generates the variation that lets you estimate uncertainty.

The idea was initially met with skepticism. Some statisticians felt it was too simple, almost like cheating — how could you learn about uncertainty by recycling the same data? Others worried about theoretical rigor. But over the following decade, Efron and his collaborators, particularly Robert Tibshirani, developed rigorous mathematical foundations showing that the bootstrap was not only valid but often superior to classical methods, especially when the data didn't follow a neat mathematical distribution.

And this is the crucial point for software estimation. Estimation errors in software do not follow a neat mathematical distribution. They are typically right-skewed: tasks are more likely to take much longer than expected than much shorter. The distribution often has heavy tails — there's always a chance of a catastrophic miss. And the shape varies from team to team and even from one task category to another. If you tried to fit a normal distribution to these errors, you would systematically underestimate the probability of large overruns, which is precisely the scenario you most need to plan for.

The bootstrap sidesteps this problem entirely. It does not need to know or guess the shape of the underlying distribution. It simply resamples from whatever your team's actual historical ratios are. If your data is skewed, the bootstrap captures that skew. If it has heavy tails, the bootstrap preserves those tails. If it is multimodal — perhaps because your team handles two different types of work with very different estimation patterns — the bootstrap reproduces that multimodality. It is, in the truest sense, a data-driven approach: the data speaks for itself.

In Horizon, the bootstrap operates on estimation accuracy ratios — the ratio of actual days to estimated days for each historical task. Rather than assuming these ratios follow any particular distribution, Horizon repeatedly samples from them (weighted by task similarity, as we'll discuss shortly) and uses the resulting distribution of sampled ratios to project the range of likely outcomes for the new task. This is Efron's 1979 idea, applied forty-five years later to a problem he likely never envisioned, working exactly as he predicted it would.

## Monte Carlo Simulation — From Nuclear Physics to Sprint Planning

The year is 1946, and the mathematician Stanislaw Ulam is recovering from encephalitis at his home in Los Angeles. To pass the time, he plays solitaire. A lot of solitaire. And at some point during those long days of shuffling and dealing, he begins to wonder: what is the probability that a particular solitaire layout will result in a winnable game?

He could try to calculate it analytically — work out the combinatorics, trace the decision trees, account for every possible sequence of moves. But the number of possible configurations is astronomical, and the rules of the game create complex dependencies that resist clean mathematical treatment. So Ulam had a different idea: what if, instead of calculating the probability, he simply played the game a hundred times and counted how often he won?

This insight — that you can estimate a quantity by simulating the process that generates it, many times, and observing the results — is the foundation of Monte Carlo simulation. Ulam recognized its potential and shared it with his colleague John von Neumann, who saw immediately how it could be applied to the problems they were both working on at Los Alamos National Laboratory. The scientists there were trying to model neutron diffusion in nuclear weapons, a problem that involved tracking particles as they scattered, absorbed, and split through layers of material. The analytical mathematics was intractable, but the process could be simulated: start a virtual neutron, roll the dice at each interaction point, and see what happens. Do it ten thousand times, and the aggregate behavior reveals the physics.

The method was given the code name "Monte Carlo" by Nicholas Metropolis, a reference to the famous casino in Monaco — an apt metaphor for a technique built on repeated random sampling. The name stuck, and over the following decades, Monte Carlo methods spread from nuclear physics to virtually every field that deals with uncertainty: finance (pricing options and assessing portfolio risk), engineering (reliability analysis and structural design), medicine (radiation therapy planning), and eventually project management.

The fundamental principle is elegant in its generality. Whenever you face a problem where the outcome depends on uncertain inputs, and the relationship between inputs and outputs is complex enough to resist analytical solution, you can often get a good answer by simulating the process many times with randomly drawn inputs and observing the distribution of outputs. You don't need to solve the equations; you just need to run the experiment.

In software estimation, the uncertain inputs are the estimation accuracy ratios — we don't know which historical ratio will best predict the current task's outcome. The relationship between input (which ratio nature will "choose") and output (how long the task will actually take) is simple multiplication, but the uncertainty about which ratio applies makes the outcome fundamentally unpredictable. Monte Carlo simulation resolves this by drawing a ratio at random (weighted by relevance), computing the resulting effort estimate, and repeating this ten thousand times. The distribution of those ten thousand outcomes is our best empirical approximation of the true probability distribution of the task's duration.

There is a mathematical guarantee underlying this approach: the law of large numbers ensures that as the number of simulations grows, the empirical distribution converges to the true distribution. Ten simulations would give you a rough sketch. A hundred, a reasonable approximation. Ten thousand, a highly stable and reliable picture. Horizon uses ten thousand iterations as its default because this is well beyond the point where adding more simulations changes the percentile estimates by any meaningful amount — a point that can be verified empirically by running the same estimation with different seeds and observing that the results barely fluctuate.

What connects Ulam's solitaire game to your sprint planning meeting is the recognition that both involve systems too complex for exact prediction but amenable to empirical approximation through repeated simulation. You cannot know in advance which historical scenario your next task will most resemble. But you can run all of them, weighted by relevance, and let the aggregate picture guide your expectations.

The Monte Carlo approach also has a property that is easy to overlook but profoundly important: it produces not just an answer but a measure of how confident that answer is. A parametric model gives you a point estimate and, if you're lucky, an error bar derived from assumptions about the data. A Monte Carlo simulation gives you the full distribution — you can read off any percentile you want, compute any summary statistic, and see with your own eyes the shape of the uncertainty. If the distribution is tight and symmetric, you know the estimate is robust. If it is wide and skewed, you know there is genuine risk of a large overrun, and you can plan accordingly. This transparency is not a technical nicety; it is the difference between an estimate that enables good decisions and one that merely feels precise.

## Weighted Sampling and the Gaussian Kernel

Imagine you are trying to estimate how long it will take to drive from your home to a restaurant you haven't visited before. You've driven to many restaurants over the years, and you have a rough mental catalog of how long those trips took. But not all of those memories are equally useful. The trip to the restaurant two blocks away tells you almost nothing about a cross-town drive. The trip to the restaurant in the same neighborhood as the new one tells you quite a lot.

This is the intuition behind weighted sampling. When you draw on past experience to forecast the future, some experiences are more relevant than others. The question is how to formalize this relevance — how to assign a number to the idea that "this past task is more like my current task than that past task."

In Horizon, relevance is defined by proximity in story points. A five-point task in the past is highly relevant when estimating a new five-point task. A three-point task is somewhat relevant. A thirteen-point task is only marginally relevant. The mathematical tool used to encode this graduated relevance is the Gaussian function — the familiar bell curve that appears throughout statistics and the natural sciences.

The Gaussian function is a natural choice for this purpose, and not by accident. It has several properties that align well with the intuition of "similarity." It is symmetric, so a task that is two points larger and a task that is two points smaller are treated as equally relevant. It is smooth, so small differences in story points lead to small differences in relevance, with no abrupt cutoffs. And it decays exponentially as the distance grows, meaning that very dissimilar tasks contribute negligibly without being excluded entirely.

The bandwidth parameter — sigma, set to two and a half story points by default — controls how quickly the relevance drops off with distance. A smaller sigma means the model is more selective, focusing almost exclusively on tasks of the same size. A larger sigma means the model is more inclusive, drawing on a broader range of historical tasks. This creates a tension that is fundamental to all statistical learning: the bias-variance tradeoff.

With a very small sigma, you use only the most relevant data, reducing bias (the estimates are based on the most similar tasks) but increasing variance (you might only have two or three tasks in that narrow window, so your estimates are noisy). With a very large sigma, you use all the data, reducing variance (more data means more stable estimates) but potentially introducing bias (dissimilar tasks may have different estimation patterns). The default value of two and a half story points represents a reasonable middle ground for teams that use Fibonacci-style point scales, where the spacing between adjacent values grows as the values get larger.

This idea of weighting data by proximity has a rich intellectual history in statistics, stretching back to the concept of kernel density estimation developed in the 1950s and 1960s. The idea there is similar: if you want to estimate the probability density of a continuous variable at a particular point, you can place a small bump (a kernel) at each data point and sum them up. The Gaussian is the most commonly used kernel, for the same reasons it works well here: smoothness, symmetry, and mathematical convenience.

In Horizon's case, the weighting serves a slightly different purpose than in kernel density estimation — it determines the sampling probabilities for the bootstrap rather than directly constructing a density estimate — but the underlying principle is the same. Local information is more valuable than global information. The past tasks that most resemble your current task are the ones that should have the most influence on your forecast.

## From Effort to Calendar Time — The Hidden Multiplier

There is a distinction that every experienced project manager knows intuitively but that estimation tools often ignore: the difference between effort and duration. Effort is how many person-days of work a task requires — hands on keyboards, minds on problems. Duration is how many calendar days pass between starting and finishing. They are related but not the same, and the gap between them is larger and more variable than most people realize.

Consider a task that requires four days of focused development work. In theory, if a developer started on Monday morning and worked on nothing else, it would be done by Thursday evening. In practice, it almost never works that way. There are meetings — sprint ceremonies, architecture reviews, one-on-ones. There is context-switching — the developer might be pulled into an incident, asked to review a colleague's pull request, or need to answer questions about a previous feature. There are weekends. There might be a holiday. The code review takes a day because the reviewer is busy. The CI pipeline is slow and the developer doesn't sit idle while waiting.

All of these forces stretch effort into calendar time, and they do so inconsistently. A task started on a Monday before a holiday weekend experiences more calendar overhead than the same task started on a Tuesday in a quiet week. A developer who is the sole expert on a particular system will face more interruptions than a developer working in a well-staffed area. The ratio of calendar days to effort days is itself a random variable, with its own mean and its own spread.

Horizon handles this by treating calendar estimation as a separate layer of simulation. For each historical task, it computes the ratio of calendar days elapsed to actual effort days worked. These ratios capture, in aggregate, all of the forces that stretch effort into duration — without needing to model any of them individually. A team that has lots of meetings will have higher calendar ratios. A team that works relatively uninterrupted will have lower ones. The model doesn't need to know why the ratio is what it is; it only needs to observe and reproduce the pattern.

The calendar estimation step then proceeds by the same Monte Carlo logic as the effort estimation. For each simulated effort outcome, Horizon independently samples a calendar ratio from the historical distribution (weighted by story-point similarity, just as before) and multiplies. This independence is deliberate and important. It means that in a single simulation, you might pair an optimistic effort outcome with a pessimistic calendar ratio, or vice versa. The result is a richer, more honest distribution of calendar outcomes that accounts for the fact that these two sources of uncertainty are not perfectly correlated.

The analogy that best captures this is travel planning. If someone asks you how long it takes to fly from New York to London, you might say about seven hours. That's the flight time — the aviation equivalent of effort days. But the actual elapsed time from leaving your apartment to arriving at your London hotel includes the taxi to the airport, security, boarding, taxiing, the flight itself, immigration, baggage claim, and the train into the city. The total is more like twelve or thirteen hours. And the variance of that total is much larger than the variance of the flight time alone, because each additional step introduces its own uncertainty. The seven-hour flight might become seven and a half, but the taxi might take forty-five minutes or ninety, and the immigration line might be ten minutes or an hour and a half.

Calendar estimation in Horizon works the same way: it takes the core effort estimate and layers on the empirical calendar overhead, preserving the full distribution of both.

It is worth noting that the calendar ratio varies not just between teams but within a single team over time. A team in the middle of a quiet quarter, with few meetings and no on-call rotations, will have calendar ratios closer to one — most effort days map nearly one-to-one to calendar days. The same team during a release crunch, with daily standups extended to an hour, three rounds of regression testing, and a rotating incident response duty, will see calendar ratios climb well above two. By drawing on the full historical record, weighted toward similar-sized tasks, Horizon captures this variability naturally. The confidence interval on the calendar estimate reflects not just the uncertainty in effort but the uncertainty in how disrupted the work period will be — a dimension that purely effort-based estimation methods miss entirely.

## Three-Point Estimation and PERT — A Cold War Legacy

In the late 1950s, the United States Navy faced a logistics challenge of staggering complexity. The Polaris missile program aimed to develop submarine-launched ballistic missiles — a weapons system that would become one of the cornerstones of Cold War deterrence. The program involved hundreds of contractors, thousands of tasks, and a timeline that was measured not just in months but in the urgency of a nuclear arms race. The Navy needed a way to plan and track this sprawling effort, and the tools available at the time were not up to the task.

The result was the Program Evaluation and Review Technique — PERT — developed by the Navy Special Projects Office in collaboration with the management consulting firm Booz Allen Hamilton. PERT introduced several ideas that would become foundational in project management, but the one that concerns us here is the three-point estimate.

The insight behind the three-point estimate is that a single number cannot capture the uncertainty inherent in a prediction. Instead, PERT asked estimators to provide three numbers for each task: an optimistic estimate (everything goes well), a most likely estimate (the normal case), and a pessimistic estimate (significant problems occur). From these three numbers, PERT computed a weighted average that leaned toward the most likely value while accounting for the extremes.

The specific weighting — one part optimistic, four parts most likely, one part pessimistic, all divided by six — has a mathematical justification that is elegant if somewhat obscure. It approximates the mean of a beta distribution that has been fitted to the three points. The beta distribution is a flexible probability distribution defined on a bounded interval, and the one-four-one weighting corresponds to the assumption that the most likely value falls near the center of the range. You don't need to know any of this to use the formula effectively; the Navy's genius was in packaging a statistically sound technique into a form that any project manager could apply with a pencil and a scrap of paper.

PERT spread rapidly from military procurement into civilian construction, aerospace engineering, and eventually software development. Its influence can be seen in the estimation practices of organizations around the world, from the critical path method that dominates construction scheduling to the planning poker sessions that agile teams use to size their backlogs.

But PERT has a limitation that becomes apparent over time. The three-point estimates that feed the formula are themselves human judgments, subject to all the biases we discussed earlier. If the optimistic estimate is already too optimistic — which, given the planning fallacy, it likely is — then the PERT average will inherit that optimism. The formula is only as good as its inputs, and its inputs come from the same flawed human intuition that motivated the formula in the first place.

This is where Horizon introduces an important refinement. Rather than asking a human to supply the optimistic, most likely, and pessimistic values, Horizon derives them from simulation. The tenth percentile of the Monte Carlo distribution serves as the optimistic estimate. The fiftieth percentile serves as the most likely. The ninetieth percentile serves as the pessimistic. These values are grounded in historical data rather than human judgment, which means they carry the biases of your team's past performance rather than the biases of the individual doing the estimating. And since past performance biases are consistent and measurable — your team has a track record you can observe — they are far more tractable than the nebulous optimism of a developer thinking about a task they haven't started yet.

The PERT weighted average that Horizon computes from these data-derived percentiles is therefore a hybrid: a Cold War-era formula filled with twenty-first-century data. It retains the simplicity and communicative power of the original three-point estimate while replacing its weakest link — the human inputs — with empirical evidence.

There is a certain poetry in this lineage. A technique born from the urgency of nuclear deterrence, refined through decades of construction and aerospace practice, now finds application in estimating how long it will take to build a search feature or redesign a login flow. The scale is different, the stakes are different, but the underlying challenge is the same: coordinating human effort under uncertainty, with limited information and real consequences for getting it wrong. The Cold War planners would recognize the problem instantly, even if they might be amused by the context.

## The Broader Landscape — How Others Solve This Problem

Horizon is one approach to a problem that the software industry has been wrestling with for decades. To understand where it fits, it helps to survey the broader landscape of estimation and forecasting techniques, each with its own philosophy, strengths, and blind spots.

The most common approach in agile teams is velocity-based forecasting. The idea is straightforward: measure how many story points the team completes per sprint, average it over several sprints, and use that velocity to predict when future work will be done. If the team averages forty points per sprint and the backlog contains two hundred points, the forecast is five sprints.

Velocity-based forecasting has the virtue of simplicity, and it works reasonably well when conditions are stable. But it has several well-known weaknesses. Velocity conflates capacity with capability — a team might have high velocity because they are effective, or because they are inflating their story point estimates to look productive. Velocity is sensitive to team composition changes, interruptions, and the inevitable variation in the mix of easy and hard tasks from sprint to sprint. And velocity forecasting operates at the portfolio level — it tells you when a collection of work might be done, not how long a specific task will take. For single-task estimation, it offers little guidance.

A more sophisticated approach, championed by practitioners like Daniel Vacanti and Troy Magennis, is flow-based Monte Carlo forecasting. Instead of using story points, this approach works with historical throughput data — how many items the team finishes per unit of time — and cycle time data — how long individual items take from start to finish. The Monte Carlo simulation randomly samples throughput values from history to project when a given number of work items will be complete. This approach has the advantage of working without story points entirely, which appeals to teams that find story points unreliable or contentious.

Flow-based forecasting and Horizon's approach are complementary rather than competing. Flow-based methods answer the question "when will this set of twenty items be done?" by simulating aggregate throughput. Horizon answers the question "how long will this specific task take?" by simulating individual task effort. A team could use both: Horizon for detailed task-level estimation during sprint planning, and flow-based forecasting for release-level planning across multiple sprints.

Steve McConnell's concept of the Cone of Uncertainty, introduced in his influential 2006 book "Software Estimation: Demystifying the Black Art," offers a complementary framework for thinking about when to estimate. The Cone of Uncertainty describes how the range of possible outcomes narrows as a project progresses. At the initial concept stage, actual costs can vary from a quarter to four times the initial estimate. By the time requirements are complete, the range narrows to about two-thirds to one and a half times. By detailed design, it's tighter still.

Horizon's approach naturally reflects the cone, though in a different way than McConnell described it. As a team accumulates more historical data — especially data from tasks similar to the one being estimated — the bootstrap distribution tightens, because there is less uncertainty about what "tasks like this" tend to do. A team with two hundred completed tasks will get tighter confidence intervals than a team with twenty, not because anyone adjusted a parameter, but because more data inherently constrains the distribution.

There is also the "No Estimates" movement, associated with practitioners like Woody Zuill and Vasco Duarte, which argues that the effort spent on estimation would be better spent on other activities — breaking work into small pieces, limiting work in progress, and measuring actual throughput. The philosophical position is that estimation is inherently wasteful and misleading, and that teams would be better served by empirical process control than by prediction.

This is a reasonable position, and Horizon does not contradict it so much as offer a middle path. The no-estimates philosophy works well for teams with mature flow practices and relatively uniform work items. But many teams operate in environments where stakeholders need forecasts — for budgeting, for coordination with other teams, for making commitments to customers. In these contexts, the question is not whether to estimate but how to do it honestly. Probabilistic estimation, grounded in historical data, is arguably the most honest form of estimation available: it tells you what the data says, including how uncertain it is, without pretending to know more than it does. Even advocates of the no-estimates approach have acknowledged that probabilistic forecasting, when done from data rather than gut feel, represents a significant improvement over traditional estimation practices.

There are also parametric approaches that try to model estimation error directly. Some practitioners fit log-normal distributions to their historical ratios, reasoning that the multiplicative nature of estimation errors (you are more likely to be off by a factor than by a fixed amount) naturally produces log-normal patterns. This works reasonably well when the assumption holds, but it introduces a fragility: if the true distribution departs from log-normality — perhaps because the team handles two very different types of work — the model can miss important features of the data. The bootstrap approach avoids this fragility by making no distributional assumptions at all, at the cost of requiring somewhat more data to achieve the same precision.

Machine learning approaches have also been explored, using features like story points, task description text, component tags, and developer assignments to predict effort. These models can potentially capture richer patterns than a story-point-only weighting scheme. But they require substantially more data to train, they are harder to interpret and explain, and they risk overfitting to patterns that reflect team composition or project phase rather than genuine estimation dynamics. For most teams, the additional complexity of a machine learning approach does not justify its marginal improvement over simpler methods, especially when the bottleneck is often data quantity rather than model sophistication.

Horizon chose its particular approach — single-task Monte Carlo based on estimation accuracy ratios, weighted by story-point similarity — because it occupies a useful sweet spot. It requires only the data that most teams already track (or can easily start tracking): story points, initial estimates, actual effort, start dates, and completion dates. It produces actionable output for the most common question teams face: "how long will this specific task take?" And it scales gracefully with data quality and quantity, getting better as the team's historical record grows.

## When Models Break — Assumptions, Failures, and Humility

The statistician George Box famously observed that all models are wrong, but some are useful. This aphorism, worn smooth by decades of repetition, remains the most important thing to understand about any quantitative model, Horizon included. A model is a simplification of reality that captures some patterns while ignoring others. The art lies in knowing which simplifications are acceptable and which are fatal — and in recognizing when the conditions that made a model useful have changed.

The most fundamental assumption underlying Horizon's approach is stationarity: the idea that your team's future estimation accuracy will resemble its past estimation accuracy. This is a reasonable assumption when the team is stable, the technology is familiar, and the processes are consistent. It becomes a dangerous assumption when any of these change significantly. A team that onboards three new junior developers will likely see its estimation accuracy shift. A team that adopts a new programming language or framework will face a learning curve that makes historical ratios unreliable. A team that switches from two-week sprints to continuous delivery will see its calendar ratios change as the rhythm of work changes.

The stationarity assumption is not unique to Horizon — every data-driven forecasting method shares it. But it is worth being explicit about, because violations can be subtle. A team might not realize that its estimation accuracy has drifted until the model's predictions start consistently missing. The corrective is straightforward: when major changes occur, consider discounting or excluding older historical data, or start accumulating fresh data under the new conditions before relying heavily on the model.

The minimum data problem is another important limitation. The bootstrap technique, for all its elegance, is only as rich as the data it draws from. If you have five historical tasks, the bootstrap can only produce combinations of those five ratios. The resulting distribution will be coarse, with gaps and clusters that reflect the specific values in your small sample rather than the true underlying pattern. This is not a flaw in the technique — it is an honest reflection of how little the data can tell you. A model that says "I don't have enough data to give you a reliable answer" is more valuable than one that gives you a precise-looking answer based on insufficient evidence.

As a rough guideline, fifteen to twenty historical tasks provide a reasonable minimum for bootstrap-based estimation. Below that, the percentile estimates become unstable — running the same estimation with a different random seed might yield noticeably different results. Above that, the estimates stabilize quickly. At fifty tasks, you have a robust foundation. At a hundred, you have excellent coverage.

Outlier sensitivity is a related concern. If one of your historical tasks was a dramatic outlier — perhaps it was blocked for weeks by an external dependency, or it turned out to involve a fundamental architectural rethink — that outlier will appear in the bootstrap pool and be sampled in proportion to its weight. With a large dataset, one outlier is diluted by many normal observations. With a small dataset, one outlier can dominate the pessimistic end of the distribution, inflating the ninetieth percentile to a degree that is not representative of typical risk. Teams should review their historical data periodically and consider whether extreme outliers represent genuine estimation risk or one-time anomalies that should be removed.

There is also the "garbage in, garbage out" consideration. Horizon scales the initial human estimate by historically observed ratios. If the initial estimate is fundamentally miscalibrated — not just slightly optimistic, but missing entire categories of work — the Monte Carlo output will faithfully reproduce that miscalibration, just with well-formed confidence intervals around it. A three-day estimate for a task that actually requires redesigning a core subsystem will produce a distribution centered around three days times the historical ratio, regardless of how inappropriate that baseline is. The model corrects for systematic bias in estimation accuracy, but it cannot correct for structural errors in the estimate itself.

This is an important philosophical point. Probabilistic estimation is not a magic wand. It does not remove the need for thoughtful human judgment about what a task involves. What it does is add a layer of empirical calibration that adjusts for the known, consistent biases in that judgment. It turns "I think this will take five days" into "tasks like this, when your team estimated them at five days, historically took between four and eight days." The human provides the signal; the model provides the calibration.

And perhaps the most honest thing a model can do is tell you when it doesn't know. When the historical dataset is small, the confidence intervals will be wide. When the target task is far from any historical task in size, the similarity weights will be spread thin, and the estimate will be driven by weakly relevant data. These are not failures of the model — they are accurate reflections of genuine uncertainty. A narrow confidence interval means the model is confident. A wide one means it isn't. Both are useful information for decision-making.

## Why This Matters

Let us return to the sprint planning meeting from the beginning. The same team, the same conference room, the same question: "How long will this take?"

But this time, the conversation goes differently. Instead of a single number offered with varying degrees of confidence, the team looks at a dashboard. The task is a five-point feature to add search functionality. The developer's gut estimate is three days. The model, drawing on seventy-five historical tasks from the team's own track record, reports that there is a ninety percent chance the effort will fall between two and a half and five days, with a most likely value of three and a half. The calendar estimate — accounting for the team's typical meeting load, code review cycles, and the inevitable Friday afternoon context switch — suggests seven calendar days at the ninetieth percentile. Five similar tasks are listed for reference, and the team can see that their past search-related features have consistently taken about fifteen percent longer than initially estimated.

The conversation that follows is qualitatively different from the old ritual. Instead of debating whether the estimate is right — an inherently unresolvable argument about a future event — the team discusses risk. "The ninetieth percentile is five days of effort, which means there's a ten percent chance it goes beyond that. Are we comfortable with that risk for this sprint?" "The calendar estimate suggests about a week. If we start it Monday, we're looking at next Monday for delivery. Does that work for the stakeholders?"

This shift — from false precision to calibrated uncertainty, from "it will take five days" to "there's an eighty percent chance it's done within six days" — changes the nature of project management in subtle but profound ways. Commitments become more honest, because they're expressed with explicit confidence levels rather than implied certainty. Risk becomes visible, because the spread between the optimistic and pessimistic estimates directly communicates how much variability the team has historically experienced. And over time, as the team accumulates data and watches the model's predictions against reality, they develop a shared understanding of their own estimation patterns that no amount of retrospective discussion could provide.

The techniques that make this possible — Kahneman's outside view, Efron's bootstrap, Ulam's Monte Carlo method, the Navy's PERT framework, the Gaussian kernel from density estimation — were each developed to solve a different problem in a different era. A psychologist studying cognitive bias. A statistician seeking distribution-free inference. A physicist modeling neutron diffusion. A military planner coordinating missile development. A mathematician characterizing probability density.

That these disparate ideas can be woven together into a coherent tool for software estimation is a testament to the generality of the underlying principles. Uncertainty is uncertainty, whether you encounter it in nuclear physics or sprint planning. The human tendency to be overconfident is the same whether you're predicting the completion date of a textbook or a software feature. And the insight that you can learn about the future by carefully studying the past — that you can replace intuition with evidence, and false certainty with honest uncertainty — applies everywhere that people need to make decisions under conditions they cannot fully predict.

Horizon is a small tool built on large ideas. Its value lies not in any single technique but in the combination: the outside view forces you to consult history rather than imagination; the bootstrap frees you from distributional assumptions; Monte Carlo simulation lets you explore the full space of possible outcomes; similarity weighting ensures you're drawing on the most relevant history; and the PERT framework packages the result into a form that supports practical decision-making. Together, they transform the estimation conversation from an exercise in optimistic guessing into an exercise in evidence-based reasoning.

And that, in the end, is what this is about. Not prediction — because the future remains stubbornly uncertain — but honesty about that uncertainty, grounded in the best evidence available. Not replacing human judgment, but giving it better raw material. Not eliminating risk, but making it visible so that teams can face it with open eyes and make the choices that serve them best.
